01   Introduction

The Sky as Story

Before telescopes, before mathematics, before writing itself, human beings looked up. Every culture on Earth independently developed stories to explain the sun, moon, stars, and the great river of light we now call the Milky Way. These myths were not childish guesses — they were the first attempts to impose meaning on a cosmos that was vast, indifferent, and overwhelming.

65kYears of Aboriginal sky knowledge
1200BCE — oldest star catalogue (Babylon)

Cosmic mythology served practical purposes: it encoded agricultural calendars, guided navigation, ordered religious festivals, and explained eclipses and meteor showers. But it also addressed deeper questions — where did we come from, what are we made of, and what happens when the stars go out?

Universal Pattern

The same constellations were independently "discovered" and named by cultures with no contact with each other. The Pleiades are referenced in Aboriginal Australian, Greek, Māori, Norse, Hindu, and Inca traditions — always as something significant, always in the sky above them all.

02   Greece & Rome

Greek & Roman

⚡ Greece · Rome

Greek cosmology began with Chaos — a formless void — from which emerged Gaia (Earth), Tartarus (Underworld), Eros (Love), Erebus (Darkness), and Nyx (Night). Every planet in our solar system still bears a Roman name, making Greek mythology the most permanently embedded of all in modern astronomy.

🌞
Helios / Sol
God of the Sun
Helios drives a golden chariot pulled by four fire-breathing horses across the sky each day, descending into the Ocean at night. His son Phaethon famously lost control — scorching the earth, an early mythological account of solar catastrophe.
🌙
Selene / Luna
Goddess of the Moon
Selene drives a silver chariot pulled by two white horses. She fell in love with the mortal Endymion, whom Zeus put into eternal sleep so she could visit him nightly — explaining the moon's nightly passage across the sky.
🌐
Atlas
Titan who bears the heavens
Condemned to hold up the celestial sphere on his shoulders for eternity — the ancient personification of the axis around which the stars revolve. The word "atlas" (map of the world) comes from his name.
🌌
The Milky Way's Origin
Hera's Spilled Milk
Hermes placed the infant Heracles at Hera's breast while she slept. When she woke and pulled away, her milk sprayed across the sky — forming the galaxy. The Greek galaxias kyklos ("milky circle") gave us the words "galaxy" and "Milky Way."

"The Milky Way, that broad white shining band — Hera's spilled milk, scattered across heaven's vault."

— Ovid, Metamorphoses
03   Scandinavia

Norse

🌲 Norse · Germanic

Norse cosmology is among the most elaborate in the ancient world. At its center stands Yggdrasil, the World Tree — an immense ash connecting nine realms, its roots reaching into the wells of fate, frost-giants, and the dead; its branches sheltering the heavens where the gods dwell in Asgard.

☀️
Sól & Máni
The Sun & Moon
Siblings driving their chariots endlessly across the sky, pursued by two wolves — Sköll chases the sun, Hati chases the moon. At Ragnarök they will finally be caught and devoured, and the sky will go dark before the new world rises.
🐍
Jörmungandr
The World Serpent
The Midgard Serpent lies coiled around the entire earth in the ocean, biting its own tail — the Ouroboros, a near-universal symbol of cyclical time, the boundary of the world, and eternal return.
❄️
Ginnungagap & Creation
Creation from Void
In the beginning there was only Ginnungagap (the void), ice from Niflheim in the north, and fire from Muspelheim in the south. Where they met, the first giant Ymir formed — creation by collision of opposites, matching modern descriptions of the early universe.
💥
Ragnarök
The Twilight of the Gods
The Norse apocalypse involves full cosmic collapse: the sun and moon eaten, stars fall, Yggdrasil shakes, the sea swallows the land — and then a new, renewed world emerges from the waters. Cyclical destruction and rebirth.

The Norse Milky Way was called Vintergatan ("Winter Road") — a path souls traveled after death, or the route of the Wild Hunt riding across the winter sky.

04   Egypt

Egyptian

𓂀 Ancient Egypt · c. 3100 BCE

Egyptian cosmology is one of the oldest on record, with texts dating to 2400 BCE and earlier. The sky itself was a goddess — Nut — arching her star-covered body over the earth, swallowing the sun each evening and giving birth to it each morning. Her body was the Milky Way.

🌅
Ra / Amun-Ra
The Sun God
Ra travels through the sky in his solar barque each day. At sunset he enters the Duat (underworld), battling the chaos serpent Apophis through twelve hours of darkness. His nightly victory and rebirth at dawn is the cosmic cycle underlying all Egyptian religion.
🌌
Nut
Goddess of the Sky
Nut's body arches over the earth; her fingertips touch the western horizon, her toes the eastern. The stars are decorations on her body. She swallows the sun at sunset and births it at dawn. Her arching form is the Milky Way.
⭐
Osiris & Orion
Death, Resurrection, & the Stars
Osiris was identified with Orion — his soul rises to the stars. The pyramids of Giza are aligned with Orion's Belt; their shafts point toward specific circumpolar stars. The pharaoh's soul would travel to the stars that never set for eternal life.
🌙
Thoth
God of the Moon, Writing, & Time
The ibis-headed Thoth governed the moon and invented writing. He measured time with the lunar calendar and recorded the weighing of souls. The moon's phases were explained as Thoth playing senet (draughts) with the moon itself.

"I am Nut, and I enfold you and your divine essence. I have given you your life. You will live forever, for you are the son of Nut."

— Pyramid Texts, c. 2400 BCE
05   Mesopotamia

Mesopotamian

𒀭 Sumer · Babylon · Assyria

Mesopotamia gave the world its first written astronomical records, its first star catalogues, and its first zodiac — all embedded in a mythological framework where the planets were gods and the sky was a divine text to be read. Our modern planet names descend directly from this tradition through Rome.

"When the skies above were not yet named, nor earth below pronounced by name, Apsu, the first one, their begetter, and maker Tiamat, who bore them all, had mixed their waters together."

— Enuma Elish, Tablet I

After the god Marduk defeats the chaos-dragon Tiamat, he splits her body in two: one half becomes the sky (holding back the upper waters), the other becomes the earth. He then creates the stars, assigning each a station and setting the year — the oldest surviving creation-by-astronomical-ordering myth.

06   India

Hindu

🕉 Hindu · Vedic · c. 1500 BCE

Hindu cosmology operates on a scale that dwarfs most other traditions. The universe cycles endlessly through vast spans of time — a kalpa (one "day of Brahma") lasts 4.32 billion years, remarkably close to the current scientific estimate for the age of the Earth.

🌊
Samudra Manthan
The Churning of the Cosmic Ocean
Gods and demons churned the primordial cosmic ocean using Mount Meru as a churning rod and the serpent Vasuki as a rope — producing the moon, the sun, Lakshmi, and amrita (the nectar of immortality). Often interpreted as a metaphor for the rotation of the galaxy.
🌑
Rahu & Ketu
The Lunar Nodes — Causers of Eclipses
The demon Svarbhanu drank the nectar of immortality. Vishnu beheaded him, but both the head (Rahu) and tail (Ketu) became immortal. They periodically swallow the sun and moon in revenge — causing eclipses — before releasing them.
☀️
Surya
The Sun God
Surya rides a golden chariot pulled by seven horses (often interpreted as the seven colors of visible light, or the seven days of the week). His charioteer Aruna is the dawn; his path encodes the solar year.
07   Indigenous Traditions

Indigenous Star Lore

🌏 Aboriginal · Māori · Navajo · Inca

Some of the most sophisticated observational astronomy in the ancient world came from indigenous cultures. Aboriginal Australians developed the world's oldest continuous astronomical tradition — at least 65,000 years of sky-watching encoded in oral traditions, songs, and ceremony.

🦅
Aboriginal Dark Constellations
Australia — 65,000+ year tradition
While most cultures saw constellations in bright stars, Aboriginal Australians also identified "dark constellations" — shapes formed by the dark dust clouds of the Milky Way. The Emu in the Sky: its body is the dark nebula between the Southern Cross and Scorpius. When the Emu constellation is overhead, emu eggs appear on the ground — a precise harvest calendar encoded in the sky.
⭐
Māori Navigation & Matariki
Polynesia — Stars as Ancestors
The Māori navigated the Pacific using star paths, ocean swells, and bird flight. Matariki (the Pleiades) marks the Māori New Year — its heliacal rising in mid-winter signals a time to remember the dead and plan for the new season. Stars are treated as ancestors.
🦊
Navajo Night Sky
Southwest North America
The Holy People arranged the stars deliberately. The Milky Way (Yikáísdáhí, "that which awaits the dawn") is a spirit road. When the trickster Coyote grew impatient during star-placing, he grabbed the blanket and threw the remaining stars up at random — creating the scattered faint star field we see today.
🌄
Inca Ceque System
Andes — Cosmic Calendar in Stone
The Inca organised their capital Cusco as a cosmic map, with 41 ceque lines radiating from the Temple of the Sun to 328 sacred sites. This encoded the solar and lunar calendar, agricultural cycles, and risings of the Pleiades — used to predict harvest yields years in advance.

"We do not see the stars as separate. They are our ancestors, looking down. We navigate by them, we pray by them, we time our planting by them."

— Attributed to Polynesian navigator tradition
08   Our Galaxy

The Milky Way Across Cultures

The great band of light stretching across the night sky — our galaxy seen edge-on from within — may be the single most universally mythologised feature of the night sky. Every culture gave it a name, a story, and a meaning. The word "galaxy" itself is a myth made permanent in language.

What This Tells Us

What is remarkable is not just the diversity of these interpretations, but the consistency of the impulse: to see pattern, to impose narrative, to connect the sky above to the world below. The scientific name — the Milky Way galaxy, from the Greek galaxias — is itself a myth made permanent in language, carrying an ancient story about a goddess's milk into every modern physics paper ever written.

"Two things fill the mind with ever-increasing wonder and awe: the starry heavens above me and the moral law within me."

— Immanuel Kant, Critique of Practical Reason (1788)
